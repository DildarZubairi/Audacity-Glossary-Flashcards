<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latency_%28audio%29"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Latency</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A short delay between an audio signal being sent and received. In computer audio this is due to <a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#adc">analog-to-digital</a> and <a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#dac">digital-to-analog</a> conversion. Most commonly refers to the delay between recording a sound and a) hearing its <a href="recording_preferences.html#Playthrough" title="Recording Preferences">playthrough</a> or b) laying it down on disk.
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Linear</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A simple, directly proportional, one-to-one, "straight-line" relationship. This term is used to contrast with <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#exponential">exponential</a></b></i>, <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#log">logarithmic</a></b></i>, or other complex relationships.
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Logarithmic</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A non-linear relationship where one item is proportional to the logarithm of the other item. So for a logarithmic <a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/fades.html" title="Fades">fade in</a>, the curve becomes "flatter" with time; a logarithmic fade-out becomes "steeper" with time. Some measures, such as <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#decibel">dB</a></b></i>, are logarithmic by definition.  See also <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#exponential">Exponential</a></b></i>.

Examples are <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#mp3">MP3</a></b></i> and <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#ogg">OGG</a></b></i>.
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_pass_filter"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Low Pass Filter</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#filter">filter</a></b></i> that lets low (bass) <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#frequency">frequencies</a></b></i> through.

<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_floor"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Noise Floor</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A level or <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#amplitude">amplitude</a></b></i> representing the amount of near-continuous background noise present in the signal. Unwanted sporadic noise such as a member of the audience coughing is noise, but it does not contribute to the noise floor.
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Band-stop_filter"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Notch filter</b> (Audacity Glossary)|A notch filter is a <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#band_stop">band-stop filter</a></b></i> with a narrow stopband (high Q factor).
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oversampling"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Oversampling</b> (Audacity Glossary)|Oversampling is the process of sampling a signal with a sampling frequency significantly higher than the Nyquist rate. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panning_(audio)"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Pan</b> (Audacity Glossary)|Panning is the spread of a sound signal (either monaural or stereophonic pairs) into a new stereo or multi-channel sound field.

<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resampling_%28audio%29"><img src="https://manual.audacityteam.org/manual/images/8/8f/wikipedia1.png" alt="Wikipedia1.png" /></a> <b>Resampling</b> (Audacity Glossary)|Converting a sampled signal from one <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#sample_rate">sample rate</a></b></i> to another without changing the length of the audio (hence without changing the playback speed or <i><b><a href="https://manual.audacityteam.org/man/glossary.html#pitch">pitch</a></b></i>).
